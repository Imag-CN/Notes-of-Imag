>[!problem] Problem 1
>Compute the first fundamental forms of the following parametrized surfaces where they are regular:
>
>**a.** $\mathbf{x}(u, v)=(a \sin u \cos v, b \sin u \sin v, c \cos u)$; ellipsoid.
>
>**b.** $\mathbf{x}(u, v)=(a u \cos v, bu \sin v, u^{2})$; elliptic paraboloid.
>
>**c.** $\mathbf{x}(u, v)=(a u \cosh v, bu \sinh v, u^{2})$; hyperbolic paraboloid.
>
>**d.** $\mathbf{x}(u, v)=(a \sinh u \cos v, b \sinh u \sin v, c \cosh u)$; hyperboloid of two sheets.

**Proof:** 
**a.** Compute derivatives:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{x}_u &= (a\cos u\cos v,\; b\cos u\sin v,\; -c\sin u), \\
\mathbf{x}_v &= (-a\sin u\sin v,\; b\sin u\cos v,\; 0).
\end{aligned}
$$
First fundamental form coefficients:
$$
\begin{aligned}
E &= a^2\cos^2 u\cos^2 v + b^2\cos^2 u\sin^2 v + c^2\sin^2 u, \\
F &= (b^2-a^2)\sin u\cos u\sin v\cos v, \\
G &= a^2\sin^2 u\sin^2 v + b^2\sin^2 u\cos^2 v.
\end{aligned}
$$
The first fundamental form:
$$
I = E\,du^2 + 2F\,du\,dv + G\,dv^2.
$$

**b.** Compute derivatives:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{x}_u &= (a\cos v,\; b\sin v,\; 2u), \\
\mathbf{x}_v &= (-au\sin v,\; bu\cos v,\; 0).
\end{aligned}
$$
First fundamental form coefficients:
$$
\begin{aligned}
E &= a^2\cos^2 v + b^2\sin^2 v + 4u^2, \\
F &= (b^2-a^2)u\sin v\cos v, \\
G &= u^2(a^2\sin^2 v + b^2\cos^2 v).
\end{aligned}
$$
The first fundamental form:
$$
I = E\,du^2 + 2F\,du\,dv + G\,dv^2.
$$

**c.**
Compute derivatives:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{x}_u &= (a\cosh v,\; b\sinh v,\; 2u), \\
\mathbf{x}_v &= (au\sinh v,\; bu\cosh v,\; 0).
\end{aligned}
$$
First fundamental form coefficients:
$$
\begin{aligned}
E &= a^2\cosh^2 v + b^2\sinh^2 v + 4u^2, \\
F &= (a^2+b^2)u\sinh v\cosh v, \\
G &= a^2u^2\sinh^2 v + b^2u^2\cosh^2 v.
\end{aligned}
$$
The first fundamental form:
$$
I = E\,du^2 + 2F\,du\,dv + G\,dv^2.
$$

**d.**
Compute derivatives:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{x}_u &= (a\cosh u\cos v,\; b\cosh u\sin v,\; c\sinh u), \\
\mathbf{x}_v &= (-a\sinh u\sin v,\; b\sinh u\cos v,\; 0).
\end{aligned}
$$
First fundamental form coefficients:
$$
\begin{aligned}
E &= a^2\cosh^2 u\cos^2 v + b^2\cosh^2 u\sin^2 v + c^2\sinh^2 u, \\
F &= (b^2-a^2)\sinh u\cosh u\sin v\cos v, \\
G &= \sinh^2 u\,(a^2\sin^2 v + b^2\cos^2 v).
\end{aligned}
$$
The first fundamental form:
$$
I = E\,du^2 + 2F\,du\,dv + G\,dv^2.
$$

>[!problem] Problem 2
>Obtain the first fundamental form of the sphere in the parametrization given by stereographic projection:
>$$
>\pi^{-1} \left\{
\begin{aligned}
x &= \frac{4u}{u^2+v^2+4}, \\
y &= \frac{4v}{u^2+v^2+4}, \\
z &= \frac{2(u^2+v^2)}{u^2+v^2+4}.
\end{aligned}
\right.
>$$

**Proof:**
Let $\rho^2 = u^2+v^2$, $D = \rho^2+4$, then:
$$
\mathbf{x}_u = \left( \frac{4(D-2u^2)}{D^2}, \; \frac{-8uv}{D^2}, \; \frac{8u}{D^2} \right),
$$
$$
\mathbf{x}_v = \left( \frac{-8uv}{D^2}, \; \frac{4(D-2v^2)}{D^2}, \; \frac{8v}{D^2} \right).
$$
First fundamental form coefficients:
$$
E = \mathbf{x}_u\cdot\mathbf{x}_u = \frac{16}{D^2}, \quad
F = \mathbf{x}_u\cdot\mathbf{x}_v = 0, \quad
G = \mathbf{x}_v\cdot\mathbf{x}_v = \frac{16}{D^2}.
$$

Therefore the first fundamental form:
$$
I = \frac{16}{(u^2+v^2+4)^2} \, (du^2 + dv^2).
$$

>[!problem] Problem 3
>Show that a surface of revolution can always be parametrized so that
>
>$$
>E = E ( v ),\quad F = 0,\quad G = 1.
>$$

**Proof:**
Let a surface of revolution $S$ be generated by rotating profile curve $\alpha(s)= (f(s), 0, g(s))$ (where $s$ is the arc length parameter) about $z$-axis:
$$
\mathbf{x}(u,s) = (f(s)\cos u, f(s)\sin u, g(s)).
$$
Compute the derivative:
$$
\mathbf{x}_u = (-f(s)\sin u, f(s)\cos u, 0),\mathbf{x}_s=(f'(s)\cos u, f'(s)\sin u, g'(s)).
$$
Thus first fundamental form coefficient:
$$
E = f'(s)^2, \quad F = 0, \quad G = f'(s)^2+g'(s)^2.
$$
Since $s$ is the arc length parameter, we have $f'(s)^2+g'(s)^2=|\alpha'(s)|^2=1$.
Therefore, we have
$$
E = E(s)\text{ (we can rename }s \text{ as }v\text{)}, \quad F = 0, \quad G = 1.
$$
Thus a surface of revolution can always be parametrized in this form.

>[!problem] Problem 4
>Let $S$ be a surface of revolution and $C$ its generating curve . Let $s$ be the arc length of $C$ and denote by $\rho = \rho(s)$ the distance to the rotation axis of the point of $C$ corresponding to $s$.
>
>**a.** (Pappus' Theorem.) Show that the area of $S$ is
>
>$$ 2\pi \int_{0}^{l} \rho(s) \, ds, $$
>
>where $l$ is the length of $C$.
>
>**b.** Apply part **a** to compute the area of a torus of revolution.

**Proof:**
**a.** Let the generating curve $C$ be parametrized by arc length $s$ as $(x(s),0,z(s))$, with $x(s)=\rho(s)>0$ the distance to rotation $z$-axis. Parameterize $S$ as
$$
\mathbf{x}(u,s)=(\rho(s)\cos u, \rho(s)\sin u, z(s)),\quad u\in[0,2\pi],\; s\in[0,l].
$$
Since $s$ is arc length, by the last problem we know that the first fundamental form coefficients are:
$$
E=\rho(s)^2, F=0, G=1.
$$
The area element is
$$
dA=\sqrt{EG-F^2}\,du\,ds = \rho(s)\,du\,ds.
$$
Integrating over $u\in[0,2\pi]$ and $s\in[0,l]$ gives
$$
A = 2\pi\int_0^l \rho(s)\,ds.
$$

**b.** Take a circle of radius $r$ centered at distance $R$ from $z$-axis:
$$
C:\,(R+r\cos\theta,0,r\sin\theta),\theta\in[0,2\pi].
$$
Let $S$ be the torus generated by $C$ (the rotation axis is $z$-axis). Then we have arc length $s=r\theta\in[0,2\pi r]$, and $\rho(s)=R+r\cos(s/r)$. By part **a** we have:
$$
A = 2\pi\int_0^{2\pi r}\left[R+r\cos\frac{s}{r}\right]ds
= 2\pi\left[Rs + r^2\sin\frac{s}{r}\right]_0^{2\pi r}
= 4\pi^2 Rr.
$$
Therefore, the area of a torus of revolution generated by a circle of radius $r$ centered at distance $R$ from the rotation axis is $4\pi^2 Rr$.

>[!problem] Problem 5
>**(Gradient on Surfaces.)** The gradient of a differentiable function $f:S\to\mathbb{R}$ is a differentiable map $\operatorname{grad}f:S\to\mathbb{R}^3$ which assigns to each point $p\in S$ a vector $\operatorname{grad}f(p)\in T_{p}(S)\subset \mathbb{R}^3$ such that
>
>$$ \langle\operatorname{grad}f(p),v\rangle_{p}=df_{p}(v)\quad\text{ for all }v\in T_{p}(S). $$
>
>Show that
>
>**a.** If $E,\;F,\;G$ are the coefficients of the first fundamental form in a parametrization $\mathbf{x}:U\subset \mathbb{R}^2\to S$, then $\operatorname{grad}f$ on $\mathbf{x}(U)$ is given by
>
>$$ \operatorname{grad}f=\frac{f_{u}G-f_{v}F}{EG-F^{2}}\,\mathbf{x}_{u}+\frac{f_{v}E-f_{u}F}{EG-F^{2}}\,\mathbf{x}_{v}. $$
>
>In particular, if $S=\mathbb{R}^2$ with coordinates $x,y$,
>
>$$ \operatorname{grad}f=f_{x}e_{1}+f_{y}e_{2}, $$
>
>where $\{e_{1},e_{2}\}$ is the canonical basis of $\mathbb{R}^{2}$ (thus, the definition agrees with the usual definition of gradient in the plane).
>
>**b.** If you let $p\in S$ be fixed and $v$ vary in the unit circle $|v|=1$ in $T_{p}(S)$, then $df_{p}(v)$ is maximum if and only if $v=\dfrac{\operatorname{grad}f}{\lVert\operatorname{grad}f\rVert}$ (thus, $\operatorname{grad}f(p)$ gives the direction of maximum variation of $f$ at $p$).
>
>**c.** If $\operatorname{grad}f\neq0$ at all points of the level curve $C=\{q\in S;\;f(q)=\text{const.}\}$, then $C$ is a regular curve on $S$ and $\operatorname{grad}f$ is normal to $C$ at all points of $C$.

**Proof:**
**a.** Let $\mathbf{x}:U\subset\mathbb{R}^2\to S$ be a parametrization. Write
$$
\operatorname{grad}f = A\mathbf{x}_u + B\mathbf{x}_v.
$$
The defining condition is $\langle\operatorname{grad}f, v\rangle_p = df_p(v)$ for all $v\in T_pS$. In coordinates, for $v = \xi\mathbf{x}_u + \eta\mathbf{x}_v$,
$$
df_p(v) = f_u\xi + f_v\eta.
$$
Also, using the first fundamental form $I = E\,du^2+2F\,du\,dv+G\,dv^2$,
$$
\langle\operatorname{grad}f, v\rangle
= (A\mathbf{x}_u+B\mathbf{x}_v)\cdot(\xi\mathbf{x}_u+\eta\mathbf{x}_v)
= (A,B)\begin{pmatrix}E&F\\ F&G\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}\xi\\ \eta\end{pmatrix}
= (EA+FB)\xi + (FA+GB)\eta.
$$
Equating coefficients of $\xi$ and $\eta$ gives
$$
\begin{cases}
EA+FB = f_u, \\[4pt]
FA+GB = f_v.
\end{cases}
$$
Solving for $A,B$ gives:
$$
A = \frac{f_u G - f_v F}{EG-F^2}, \quad
B = \frac{f_v E - f_u F}{EG-F^2}.
$$
Hence
$$
\operatorname{grad}f = \frac{f_u G - f_v F}{EG-F^2}\,\mathbf{x}_u
+ \frac{f_v E - f_u F}{EG-F^2}\,\mathbf{x}_v.
$$

In particular, when $S=\mathbb{R}^2$ with coordinates $x,y$, we have $E=G=1$, $F=0$, $\mathbf{x}_u=e_1$, $\mathbf{x}_v=e_2$, so
$$
\operatorname{grad}f = f_x e_1 + f_y e_2.
$$

**b.** Fix $p\in S$. For $v\in T_pS$ with $|v|^2=\langle v,v\rangle_p=1$, we have
$$
df_p(v) = \langle \operatorname{grad}f, v\rangle_p.
$$
By the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality,
$$
|\langle \operatorname{grad}f, v\rangle_p|
\le \|\operatorname{grad}f\|\,\|v\| = \|\operatorname{grad}f\|.
$$
Equality holds iff $v$ is proportional to $\operatorname{grad}f$, i.e. $v = \lambda \operatorname{grad}f$ for some $\lambda>0$ (to maximize, not just give absolute maximum). Since $\|v\|=1$, we get $\lambda = 1/\|\operatorname{grad}f\|$, provided $\operatorname{grad}f\neq0$. Thus
$$
df_p(v) \text{ is maximum} \iff v = \frac{\operatorname{grad}f}{\|\operatorname{grad}f\|}.
$$
Hence $\operatorname{grad}f(p)$ gives the direction of maximum increase of $f$ at $p$.

**c.** Let $C = \{ q\in S : f(q)=c\}$, $\operatorname{grad}f\neq0$ on $C$. Take a regular parametrization $\alpha(t)\subset C$. Then $f(\alpha(t))=c$ constant, so
$$
0 = \frac{d}{dt}f(\alpha(t)) = df_{\alpha(t)}(\alpha'(t))
= \langle \operatorname{grad}f(\alpha(t)), \alpha'(t)\rangle.
$$
Thus $\operatorname{grad}f(p)$ is orthogonal to $\alpha'(t)$ for every $p=\alpha(t)\in C$, i.e. $\operatorname{grad}f$ is normal to $C$ at each point of $C$.

The condition $\operatorname{grad}f\neq0$ and the implicit function theorem guarantee that $C$ is a regular curve on $S$.

>[!problem] Problem 6
>Show that if a surface is tangent to a plane along a curve, then the points of this curve are either parabolic or planar.

**Proof:**
Let $S$ be a surface and $\Pi$ a plane tangent to $S$ along a regular curve $C \subset S \cap \Pi$.

Since $\Pi$ is tangent to $S$ at each $p \in C$, the unit normal $N(p)$ to $S$ is constant (equal to the unit normal of $\Pi$) along $C$. Thus $dN_p(v)=0$ for any $v \in T_pC$.

Choose $e_1$ tangent to $C$ and $e_2$ tangent to $S$ but orthogonal to $C$. Then
$$
dN_p(e_1) = 0, \quad dN_p(e_2) = k_2 e_2,
$$
where $k_2$ is the normal curvature in the $e_2$ direction. The matrix of $dN_p$ in basis $\{e_1, e_2\}$ is
$$
\begin{pmatrix}0&0\\ 0&k_2\end{pmatrix}.
$$
Hence the Gaussian curvature is
$$
K(p) = \det(dN_p) = 0.
$$
By definition we have:
- If $k_2=0$ as well, then $dN_p=0$, the point $p$ is planar.
- If $k_2\neq 0$, then $dN_p\neq 0$, the point $p$ is parabolic.

Thus every point of $C$ is either parabolic or planar.

>[!problem] Problem 7
>Let $C\subset S$ be a regular curve on a surface $S$ with Gaussian curvature $K>0$. Show that the curvature $k$ of $C$ at $p$ satisfies
>
>$$ k \ge \min \left( |k_{1}|,\,|k_{2}| \right) $$
>
>where $k_{1}$ and $k_{2}$ are the principal curvatures of $S$ at $p$.

**Proof:**
Let $k_1, k_2$ be principal curvatures at $p$, with $K=k_1k_2>0$, then $k_1,k_2$ same sign and nonzero.

Let $t$ be unit tangent of $C$ at $p$, $n$ principal normal of $C$, $N$ surface normal, $\phi$ angle between $N$ and $n$.

By Meusnier: $k_n = k\cos\phi$, where $k_n$ is normal curvature in direction $t$. So $|k_n| \le |k|=k$.

By Euler’s formula: $k_n = k_1\cos^2\theta + k_2\sin^2\theta$, $\theta$ angle from $t$ to $k_1$-direction.

Since $k_1,k_2$ have same sign, $k_n$ lies between $k_1$ and $k_2$, so $|k_n| \ge \min(|k_1|,|k_2|)$.

Therefore:
$$
k \ge |k_n| \ge \min(|k_1|,|k_2|).
$$

>[!problem] Problem 8
>Show that the mean curvature $H$ at $p\in S$ is given by
>
>$$ H=\frac{1}{\pi}\int_{0}^{\pi} k_{n}(\theta)\,d\theta, $$
>
>where $k_{n}(\theta)$ is the normal curvature at $p$ along a direction making an angle $\theta$ with a fixed direction.

**Proof:**
Let $e_1, e_2$ be principal directions at $p$, with principal curvatures $k_1, k_2$.

Given a unit vector $v_\theta$ in $T_pS$ making angle $\theta$ with $e_1$, Euler’s formula gives
$$
k_n(\theta) = k_1\cos^2\theta + k_2\sin^2\theta.
$$
Compute the integral
$$
\frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^\pi k_n(\theta)\,d\theta
= \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^\pi \big( k_1\cos^2\theta + k_2\sin^2\theta \big)\,d\theta.
$$
Using $\cos^2\theta = \frac{1+\cos2\theta}{2}$, $\sin^2\theta = \frac{1-\cos2\theta}{2}$, we have
$$
k_1\cos^2\theta + k_2\sin^2\theta
= \frac{k_1+k_2}{2} + \frac{k_1-k_2}{2}\cos2\theta.
$$
Integrate over $[0,\pi]$:
$$
\int_0^\pi \cos2\theta\,d\theta = 0,
\quad
\int_0^\pi 1\,d\theta = \pi.
$$

Thus
$$
\frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^\pi k_n(\theta)\,d\theta
= \frac{1}{\pi}\left[0+ \frac{k_1+k_2}{2} \cdot \pi \right]
= \frac{k_1+k_2}{2} = H(p).
$$

Therefore
$$
H = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^{\pi} k_n(\theta)\,d\theta.
$$

>[!problem] Problem 9
>Show that the sum of the normal curvatures for any pair of orthogonal directions, at a point $p\in S$, is constant.

**Proof:**
Let $k_1, k_2$ be the principal curvatures at $p\in S$.

Let $v_1$ be a unit tangent vector making angle $\theta$ with the first principal direction $e_1$. Then its normal curvature is
$$
k_{n1} = k_1\cos^2\theta + k_2\sin^2\theta.
$$
Let $v_2$ be orthogonal to $v_1$ (so angle $\theta+\pi/2$ with $e_1$). Its normal curvature is
$$
k_{n2} = k_1\cos^2\left(\theta+\frac{\pi}{2}\right) + k_2\sin^2\left(\theta+\frac{\pi}{2}\right)= k_1\sin^2\theta + k_2\cos^2\theta.
$$
Hence
$$
\begin{align}
k_{n1} + k_{n2} &= (k_1\cos^2\theta + k_2\sin^2\theta) + (k_1\sin^2\theta + k_2\cos^2\theta)\\
&= k_1(\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta) + k_2(\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta)\\
&= k_1 + k_2.
\end{align}
$$
Since $k_1+k_2 = 2H(p)$ (twice the mean curvature), which is independent of $\theta$, the sum is constant for any pair of orthogonal directions.

>[!problem] Problem10
>Describe the region of the unit sphere covered by the image of the Gauss map of the following surfaces:
>
>**a.** Paraboloid of revolution $z=x^{2}+y^{2}$.
>
>**b.** Hyperboloid of revolution $x^{2}+y^{2}-z^{2}=1$.
>
>**c.** Catenoid $x^{2}+y^{2}=\cosh^{2}z$.

**Proof:**
**a.** Parametrize by $\mathbf{x}(u,v)=(u\cos v,u\sin v,u^2)$, $u\ge0$. Compute normal:
$$
N=\frac{(-2u\cos v,-2u\sin v,1)}{\sqrt{4u^2+1}}.
$$
As $u\to\infty$, $N\to(-\cos v,-\sin v,0)$ (equator). As $u\to0$, $N\to(0,0,1)$ (north pole).  

Therefore, the image of Gauss map is the upper hemisphere $\{z_N>0\}$.

**b.** Parametrize by $\mathbf{x}(u,v)=(\cosh u\cos v,\cosh u\sin v,\sinh u)$. Compute normal:
$$
N=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\cosh 2u}}(-\cosh u\cos v,-\cosh u\sin v,\sinh u).
$$
Write $t=\sinh u$, then
$$
z_N=\frac{t}{\sqrt{1+2t^2}},\quad t\in\mathbb{R}.
$$
Range of $z_N$ is $(-1/\sqrt{2},1/\sqrt{2})$.  

Therefore, the image of Gauss map is an open band $\{|z_N|<1/\sqrt{2}\}$ on the sphere.

**c.** Parametrize by $\mathbf{x}(u,v)=(\cosh u\cos v,\cosh u\sin v,u)$. Compute normal:
$$
N=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\cosh 2u}}(-\cos v,-\sin v,\sinh u).
$$
With $t=\sinh u$,
$$
z_N=\frac{t}{\sqrt{1+2t^2}},\quad t\in\mathbb{R}.
$$
Range of $z_N$ is $(-1/\sqrt{2},1/\sqrt{2})$. 

Therefore, the image of Gauss map is an open band $\{|z_N|<1/\sqrt{2}\}$.

>[!problem] Problem 11
>Prove that
>
>**a.** The image $N\circ\alpha$ by the Gauss map $N\colon S\to S^{2}$ of a parametrized regular curve $\alpha\colon I\longrightarrow S$ which contains no planar or parabolic points is a parametrized regular curve on the sphere $S^{2}$ (called the spherical image of $\alpha$).
>
>**b.** If $C=\alpha(I)$ is a line of curvature, and $k$ is its curvature at $p$, then
>
>$$ k=|k_{n}k_{N}|, $$
>
>where $k_{n}$ is the normal curvature at $p$ along the tangent line of $C$ and $k_{N}$ is the curvature of the spherical image $N(C)\subset S^{2}$ at $N(p)$.

**Proof:**
**a.** Let $\alpha\colon I \to S$ be a regular curve with no planar or parabolic points. Then $dN_p$ is invertible for $p\in\alpha(I)$ because $K(p)=k_1k_2\neq0$ (since $K=0$ at planar/parabolic points). Also $\alpha'(t)\neq0$.

Define $\beta = N\circ\alpha\colon I\to S^2$. Then
$$
\beta'(t) = dN_{\alpha(t)}(\alpha'(t)).
$$
Since $dN_p$ is invertible and $\alpha'(t)\neq0$, we have $\beta'(t)\neq0$. Hence $\beta$ is a regular parametrized curve on $S^2$, the spherical image of $\alpha$.

**b.** Suppose $C=\alpha(I)$ is a line of curvature. Then $\alpha'(t)$ is a principal direction at $p=\alpha(t)$. Let $k_1$ be the principal curvature in that direction, so $k_n=k_1$.

We have $\beta = N\circ\alpha$, and since $\alpha'(t)$ is principal,
$$
\beta'(t) = dN_p(\alpha'(t)) = k_1 \alpha'(t).
$$
Differentiate again:
$$
\beta''(t) = k_1\alpha''(t) + k_1'\alpha'(t).
$$
The curvature $k_N$ of $\beta$ at $N(p)$ is given by
$$
k_N = \frac{\|\beta'(t)\times\beta''(t)\|}{\|\beta'(t)\|^3}.
$$
Compute cross product:
$$
\beta'(t)\times\beta''(t)
= k_1\alpha'(t) \times (k_1\alpha''(t)+k_1'\alpha'(t))
= k_1^2\, \alpha'(t)\times\alpha''(t).
$$
Thus
$$
\|\beta'(t)\times\beta''(t)\| = k_1^2 \|\alpha'(t)\times\alpha''(t)\|.
$$

Also $\|\beta'(t)\| = |k_1|\|\alpha'(t)\|$ and $\|\alpha'(t)\|=1$ (parametrized by arc length assumed for simplicity).

Therefore
$$
k_N = \frac{k_1^2 \|\alpha'(t)\times\alpha''(t)\|}{|k_1|^3}
= \frac{1}{|k_1|} \|\alpha'(t)\times\alpha''(t)\|
= \frac{k}{|k_1|},
$$
where $k=\|\alpha''(t)\|$ is curvature of $\alpha$ (since $\alpha'(t)\cdot\alpha''(t)=0$ for arc length param).

Hence $k = |k_1|\,k_N = |k_n k_N|$.
