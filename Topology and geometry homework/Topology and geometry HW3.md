>[!problem] Problem 1
>Let $X$ be a topological space and $A$ be a subset of $X$. Suppose that for every $x\in A$, there is an open set $U$ containing $x$ such that $U \subset A$. Show that $A$ is open.

For all $x\in A$, let $U_x$ be an open set containing $x$ such that $U_x \subset A$.
Then $\bigcup_{x\in A}U_x$ is an open set.
$$U_x \subset A\Rightarrow \bigcup_{x\in A}U_x\subseteq A,\quad x\in U_x\Rightarrow A\subseteq\bigcup_{x\in A}U_x\subseteq A.$$
Thus $A=\bigcup_{x\in A}U_x$ is open.

>[!problem] Problem 2
>Let $\mathcal{T}_{1},\mathcal{T}_{2}$ be two topologies on a same set $X$. Show that $\mathcal{T}_{1}\cap\mathcal{T}_{2}$ is also a topology on $X$. Give an example to show that $\mathcal{T}_{1}\cup\mathcal{T}_{2}$ may not be a topology.

Let $\mathcal{T}_{1}$ and $\mathcal{T}_{2}$ be topologies on the same set $X$. We verify that $\mathcal{T}_{1} \cap \mathcal{T}_{2}$ satisfies the three axioms of a topology.
#### Empty set and whole space 
Since $\mathcal{T}_{1}$ and $\mathcal{T}_{2}$ are topologies, we have $\varnothing \in \mathcal{T}_{1}$ and $\varnothing \in \mathcal{T}_{2}$, so $\varnothing \in \mathcal{T}_{1} \cap \mathcal{T}_{2}$.  
Similarly, $X \in \mathcal{T}_{1}$ and $X \in \mathcal{T}_{2}$, so $X \in \mathcal{T}_{1} \cap \mathcal{T}_{2}$.
#### Finite intersections
Let $U, V \in \mathcal{T}_{1} \cap \mathcal{T}_{2}$. Then $U, V \in \mathcal{T}_{1}$ and $U, V \in \mathcal{T}_{2}$.  
Since $\mathcal{T}_{1}$ is a topology, $U \cap V \in \mathcal{T}_{1}$.  
Since $\mathcal{T}_{2}$ is a topology, $U \cap V \in \mathcal{T}_{2}$.  
Therefore, $U \cap V \in \mathcal{T}_{1} \cap \mathcal{T}_{2}$.
#### Arbitrary unions
Let $\{U_{\alpha}\}_{\alpha \in I}$ be a collection of sets in $\mathcal{T}_{1} \cap \mathcal{T}_{2}$.  
Then each $U_{\alpha} \in \mathcal{T}_{1}$ and each $U_{\alpha} \in \mathcal{T}_{2}$.  
Since $\mathcal{T}_{1}$ is a topology, $\bigcup_{\alpha \in I} U_{\alpha} \in \mathcal{T}_{1}$.  
Since $\mathcal{T}_{2}$ is a topology, $\bigcup_{\alpha \in I} U_{\alpha} \in \mathcal{T}_{2}$.  
Therefore, $\bigcup_{\alpha \in I} U_{\alpha} \in \mathcal{T}_{1} \cap \mathcal{T}_{2}$.

Since all three axioms are satisfied, $\mathcal{T}_{1} \cap \mathcal{T}_{2}$ is a topology on $X$.
___
#### Example showing $\mathcal{T}_{1} \cup \mathcal{T}_{2}$ may not be a topology
Let $X = \{1,2,3\}$ and define:
$$
\mathcal{T}_{1} = \{\varnothing, \{a\}, X\},\mathcal{T}_{2} = \{\varnothing, \{b\}, X\}
$$
Both $\mathcal{T}_{1}$ and $\mathcal{T}_{2}$ are topologies on $X$.
However, $\mathcal{T}_{1} \cup \mathcal{T}_{2} = \{\varnothing, \{a\}, \{b\}, X\}$ is not a topology because $\{a\},\{b\} \in \mathcal{T}_{1} \cup \mathcal{T}_{2}$ , but $\{a\} \cup \{b\} = \{a, b\} \notin \mathcal{T}_{1} \cup \mathcal{T}_{2}$.

>[!problem] Problem 3
>Show that if $\mathcal{A}$ is a basis for a topology on $X$, then the topology generated by $\mathcal{A}$ equals the intersection of all collections of topologies on $X$ that contain $\mathcal{A}$.
>(The wording of the original problem has been slightly modified to reduce ambiguity.)

Denote by $\tau_{\mathcal{A}}$ the topology generated by $\mathcal{A}$, and let $Y$ be the collection of all topologies on $X$ that contain $\mathcal{A}$. Define $$
\tau_{\cap} = \bigcap_{\tau \in Y} \tau.$$By definition we have $\tau_{\cap} \subseteq \tau_{\mathcal{A}}$, thus we only have to show $\tau_{\mathcal{A}} \subseteq \tau_{\cap}$.
Take any $U \in \tau_{\mathcal{A}}$, $U$ is a union of basis elements from $\mathcal{A}$.
For any $\tau \in Y$, since $\mathcal{A} \subseteq \tau$ and $\tau$ is a topology (hence closed under unions), $U$ must be in $\tau$.
Thus $U \in \tau$ for all $\tau \in Y$, so $U \in \bigcap_{\tau \in Y} \tau = \tau_{\cap}$.
Therefore, $\tau_{\mathcal{A}} \subseteq \tau_{\cap}$, so $\tau_{\mathcal{A}} = \tau_{\cap}$.

>[!problem] Problem 4
>Show that the countable collection $$\{(a,b)\times(c,d)\mid a<b\text{ and }c<d,\text{ and }a,b,c,d\text{ are rational}\} $$
>is a basis for $\mathbb{R}^{2}$ (endowed with the standard Euclidean topology).

We prove a lemma first:
>[!tip] Lemma
>$\mathcal{A}$ is a basis for a topology on $X$. $\mathcal{A_0}\in\mathcal{P}(X)$ and for any $A_0\in \mathcal{A_0}$, there exists some $A_{\alpha}\in\mathcal{A},s.t. \bigcup_{\alpha}A_{\alpha}=A_0$. Show that $\mathcal{B}=\mathcal{A}\cup \mathcal{A_0}$ is also a basis and generate the same topology as $\mathcal{A}$ does.

Since $\mathcal{A}$ is a basis, it covers $X$.
Because $\mathcal{A} \subseteq \mathcal{B}$, it follows immediately that $\mathcal{B}$ also covers $X$.

Let $x \in B_1 \cap B_2$, where $B_1, B_2 \in \mathcal{B}$.
Then for some $A_1, A_2 \in \mathcal{A}$, we have $x \in A_1 \subseteq B_1$ and $x \in A_2 \subseteq B_2$.  
Thus for some $A_3\in\mathcal{A}\subseteq\mathcal{B}$, we have $x\in A_3\subseteq A_1\cap A_2 \subseteq B_1\cap B_2$.

Therefore, $\mathcal{B}$ is a basis.

$\mathcal{A} \subseteq \mathcal{B}$ implies $\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{A}} \subseteq \mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}$.
$\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{A}}$ is obtained by closing $A$ under arbitrary unions, and every element in $\mathcal{B}$ can be obtained by taking unions of elements from $\mathcal{A}$, therefore $\mathcal{B}\subseteq\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{A}}$.
$\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{A}}$ is closed under arbitrary union, thus $\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}\subseteq\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{A}}$.

Therefore, $\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}=\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{A}}$.

Denote $\mathcal{A}=\{(a,b)\times(c,d)\mid a<b\text{ and }c<d,\text{ and }a,b,c,d\text{ are rational}\}$.
We already know that $\mathcal{B}=\{(a,b)\times(c,d)\mid a<b\text{ and }c<d,\text{ and }a,b,c,d\in\mathbb{R}\}$ is a basis and generates the standard Euclidean topology.
By definition we have $\mathcal{B}\subseteq\mathcal{A}$.
For any $(a,b)\times(c,d)\in\mathcal{B}$, let $\{a_n\}$ be a decreasing rational sequence with limit $a$,  $\{b_n\}$ be a increasing rational sequence with limit $b$,  $\{c_n\}$ be a decreasing rational sequence with limit $c$,  $\{d_n\}$ be a decreasing rational sequence with limit $d$, and $a_1<b_1,c_1<d_1$.
Then $(a,b)\times(c,d)=\bigcup_{n\in \mathbb{Z}^+}(a_n,b_n)\times(c_n,d_n)$
Thus by lemma, $\mathcal{B}$ is a basis and $\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}=\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{A}}$.

>[!problem] Problem 7
>Let $(X,d)$ be a metric space. Suppose there is a basis that consists of only finitely many elements. Show that $X$ is finite. Give an example to show the statement need not be true if $X$ is a general topological space (instead of being a metric space).

$X$ has a finite basis, so there is only a limited number of ways to take unions of its elements, which implies the finiteness of $\mathcal{T}_d$.
Assume for contradiction that $X$ is infinite.

**Case 1**: $X$ has a limit point $p$.
Since $p$ is a limit point, $B(p,r)\setminus\{p\}\neq\varnothing$ for any positive real number $r$. 
We recursively define a sequence of open sets:
- $U_1=B(p,1)$,
- $U_{n+1}=B(p,d(p,u_n))$, where $u_n$ is an arbitrary point in $U_n$.
Since $u_n\in U_n$ but $u_n\notin U_{n+k}$ for any positive integer $k$, $U_n\neq U_{n+k}$.
Therefore, the collection of $U_n$ is infinite, contradicting to the finiteness of $\mathcal{T}_d$.

**Case 2**: $X$ has no limit points.
This is to say every point $p$ in $X$ is isolated, $i.e.B(p,r)=\{p\}$ for a sufficiently small real number $r$.
Therefore, every singleton set $\{p\}$ in $X$ is open, also contradicting to the finiteness of $\mathcal{T}_d$.

In conclusion, $X$ is finite.
#### Counterexample in a general topological space
But $X$ be a infinite set equipped with trivial topology.
Then $\{X\}$ is a basis, which consists of only one element.
