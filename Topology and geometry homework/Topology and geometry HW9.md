>[!problem] Problem 1
>Show that the volume $V$ of a parallelepiped generated by three linearly independent vectors $u, v, w\in R^{3}$ is given by $V=|(u\times v)\cdot w|$, and introduce an oriented volume in $R^{3}$.
>
>Prove that
>
>$$V^{2}=\begin{vmatrix} u\cdot u&u\cdot v&u\cdot w\\ v\cdot u&v\cdot v&v\cdot w\\ w\cdot u&w\cdot v&w\cdot w\end{vmatrix}.$$

**Proof:**
Using the identity $(a \times b) \cdot c = \det(a, b, c)$, we get:
$$V^2=((u \times v) \cdot w)^2 = (\det(u, v, w))^2.$$
Let $A = (u, v, w)$, then:
$$\det(u, v, w)^2 =\det A^T\cdot \det A= \det(A^TA).$$
The matrix $A^T A$ is:
$$A A^T = \begin{pmatrix} u\cdot u & u\cdot v & u\cdot w \\ v\cdot u & v\cdot v & v\cdot w \\ w\cdot u & w\cdot v & w\cdot w \end{pmatrix}.$$

Therefore,
$$V^2 = \det \begin{pmatrix} u\cdot u & u\cdot v & u\cdot w \\ v\cdot u & v\cdot v & v\cdot w \\ w\cdot u & w\cdot v & w\cdot w \end{pmatrix}.$$

>[!problem] Problem 2
>Let $u(t)=(u_{1}(t),u_{2}(t),u_{3}(t))$ and $v(t)=(v_{1}(t),v_{2}(t),v_{3}(t))$ be differentiable maps from the interval $(a,b)$ into $R^{3}$. If the derivatives $u^{\prime}(t)$ and $v^{\prime}(t)$ satisfy the conditions
>
>$$u^{\prime}(t)=a u(t)+b v(t),\quad v^{\prime}(t)=c u(t)-a v(t),$$
>
>where $a$, $b$, and $c$ are constants, show that $u(t)\times v(t)$ is a constant vector.

**Proof:**
Let $\mathbf{C}(t) = u(t) \times v(t)$. Differentiate using the product rule:
$$\frac{d\mathbf{C}}{dt} = u'(t) \times v(t) + u(t) \times v'(t).$$
Substitute the given derivatives:
$$\frac{d\mathbf{C}}{dt} = (a u + b v) \times v + u \times (c u - a v) = a(u \times v) + b(v \times v) + c(u \times u) - a(u \times v).$$
Since $v \times v = 0$ and $u \times u = 0$, we get:
$$\frac{d\mathbf{C}}{dt} = a(u \times v) - a(u \times v) = 0.$$
Therefore, $\mathbf{C}(t)$ is constant.

>[!problem] Problem 3
>Show that the torsion $\tau$ of $\alpha$ is given by
>
>$$\tau(s)=-\frac{\alpha^{\prime}(s)\times \alpha^{\prime\prime}(s)\cdot\alpha^{\prime\prime\prime}(s)}{|k(s)|^{2}}.$$

**Proof:**
Compute:
$$\begin{align}
\alpha'(s) \times \alpha''(s) \cdot \alpha'''(s) &= (T \times  kN) \cdot (k'N + kN') \\
&= kB \cdot (-k^2T + k'N - k\tau B) \\
&= kB \cdot (k\tau B) = k^2\tau
\end{align}
$$
Therefore:
$$\tau(s)=-\frac{\alpha^{\prime}(s)\times \alpha^{\prime\prime}(s)\cdot\alpha^{\prime\prime\prime}(s)}{|k(s)|^{2}}.$$

>[!problem] Problem 4
>Assume that all normals of a parametrized curve pass through a fixed point.
>
>Prove that the trace of the curve is contained in a circle.

**Proof:**
Let $p_0$ be the fixed point through which all normals pass. The normal line at $\alpha(s)$ has direction $N(s)$ and passes through $p_0$, so:
$$p_0 - \alpha(s) = \lambda(s) N(s) \quad \text{for some scalar } \lambda(s).$$
$\lambda(s)$ cannot be always zero, otherwise we would have $\alpha(s)=p_{0}$.

Differentiate both sides with respect to $s$:
$$-\alpha'(s) = \lambda'(s) N(s) + \lambda(s) N'(s).$$
Thus:
$$-T(s) = -\lambda(s)k(s)T(s) + \lambda'(s) N(s) - \lambda(s)\tau(s) B(s).$$
Dot both sides with $N(s)$ yields:
$$0 = \lambda'(s)\Rightarrow\lambda(s)\text{ is a non-zero constant.}$$
Dot both sides with $B(s)$ yields:
$$
\lambda(s)\tau(s)=0\Rightarrow \tau(s)=0.
$$
Dot both sides with $T(s)$ yields:
$$
-1=-\lambda(s)k(s)\Rightarrow k(s)=\dfrac{1}{\lambda(s)} \text{ is a non-zero constant}
$$
A circle with radius $|\lambda|$ satisfied the torsion and curvature requirement. Since curvature and torsion uniquely determine a curve, the trace of the curve must lie on a circle with radius $|\lambda|$.

>[!problem] Problem 5
>Let $\alpha: I\longrightarrow R^{2}$ be a regular parametrized plane curve (arbitrary parameter), and define $n=n(t)$ and $k=k(t)$ as in Remark 1. Assume that $k(t)\neq 0$, $t\in I$. In this situation, the curve
>
>$$\beta(t)=\alpha(t)+\frac{1}{k(t)}n(t),\quad t\in I,$$
>
>is called the evolute of $\alpha$.
>
>a. Show that the tangent at $t$ of the evolute of $\alpha$ is the normal to $\alpha$ at $t$.
>
>b. Consider the normal lines of $\alpha$ at two neighboring points $t_{1},t_{2},t_{1}\neq t_{2}$. Let $t_{1}$ approach $t_{2}$ and show that the intersection points of the normals converge to a point on the trace of the evolute of $\alpha$.

**Proof:**
**a.** Use arc length parameter and differentiate:
$$\beta'(s) = \alpha'(s) + \left(\frac{1}{k(s)}\right)' n(s) + \frac{1}{k(s)} n'(s).$$
Then:
$$\beta'(s) = t(s) + \left(\frac{1}{k(s)}\right)' n(t) + \frac{1}{k(s)} (-k(s)t(s)) =\left(\frac{1}{k(s)}\right)' n(s)$$
Here $n'(s)=-k(s)t(s)$ is because $\alpha$ is a plane curve, whose torsion is zero.

So $\beta'(s)$ is parallel to $n(s)$, the conclusion holds.
**b.** Let the intersection point be $P$. From the definition of the normal lines, we have:
$$
P = \alpha(t_1) + s_1 n(t_1) = \alpha(t_2) + s_2 n(t_2). \quad (1)
$$
From **a.**, the tangent vector to the evolute $\beta$ is $\beta'(t) = \left(\frac{1}{k(t)}\right)' n(t)$. Since $k(t) \neq 0$ for a regular curve, $\beta'(t)$ is parallel to $n(t)$. This implies that the evolute $\beta$ is the locus of centers of curvature. The normal line at a point $\alpha(t)$ is the line through $\alpha(t)$ in the direction of $n(t)$, and the center of curvature lies on this line at a distance $1/k(t)$.

Therefore, the intersection point $P$ of two nearby normals corresponds to the intersection of lines passing through $\alpha(t_i)$ directed along $n(t_i)$. In the limit as $t_2 \to t_1$, the two normals converge to the same line, and their intersection point converges to the point on that line that is the center of curvature for the curve at $t_1$, i.e., $\beta(t_1)$. Formally, as $t_2 \to t_1$, we have $s_1, s_2 \to 1/k(t_1)$, so
$$
\lim_{t_2 \to t_1} P = \alpha(t_1) + \frac{1}{k(t_1)} n(t_1) = \beta(t_1).
$$
Thus, the intersection points converge to a point on the evolute.

>[!problem] Problem 6
>The trace of the parametrized curve (arbitrary parameter)
>
>$$\alpha(t)=(t,\cosh t),\quad t\in R,$$
>
>is called the catenary.
>
>a. Show that the signed curvature (cf. Remark 1) of the catenary is
>
>$$k(t)=\frac{1}{\cosh ^{2} t}.$$
>
>b. Show that the evolute (cf. Exercise 7) of the catenary is
>
>$$\beta(t)=(t-\sinh t\cosh t,2\cosh t).$$

**Proof:**
**a.** Compute:  
$$\alpha'(t) = (1, \sinh t)\alpha''(t) = (0, \cosh t).$$
Use the formula for signed curvature:
$$k(t) = \frac{1 \cdot \cosh t - 0}{(1 + \sinh^2 t)^{3/2}} = \frac{\cosh t}{\cosh^3 t} = \frac{1}{\cosh^2 t}.$$
**b.** The unit tangent vector:  
$$T(t) = \frac{\alpha'(t)}{|\alpha'(t)|} = \left( \frac{1}{\cosh t}, \frac{\sinh t}{\cosh t} \right) = (\operatorname{sech} t, \tanh t).$$
The unit normal vector:  
$$n(t) = (-\tanh t, \operatorname{sech} t).$$
Curvature $k(t) = 1/\cosh^2 t$, so the radius of curvature is $1/k(t) = \cosh^2 t$.  
The evolute is:  
$$\beta(t) = \alpha(t) + \frac{1}{k(t)}n(t) = (t, \cosh t) + \cosh^2 t (-\tanh t, \operatorname{sech} t).$$
Simplify:
$$\beta(t) = \left(t - \cosh^2 t \cdot \frac{\sinh t}{\cosh t},\; \cosh t + \cosh^2 t \cdot \frac{1}{\cosh t}\right) = (t - \sinh t \cosh t,\; 2\cosh t).$$

>[!problem] Problem 7
>Consider the map
>
>$$ \alpha(t)=\left\{\begin{array}{ll}
>\left(t, 0,e^{-1/t^{2}}\right), &t>0\\
>\left(t, e^{-1/t^{2}},0\right), &t<0\\
>(0,0,0), &t=0
>\end{array}\right. $$
>
>a. Prove that $\alpha$ is a differentiable curve.
>
>b. Prove that $\alpha$ is regular for all $t$ and that the curvature $k(t)\neq 0$ , for $t\neq 0$ , $t\neq\pm\sqrt{2/3}$ , and $k(0)=0$ .
>
>c. Show that the limit of the osculating planes as $t\rightarrow 0,t>0$ , is the plane $y=0$ but that the limit of the osculating planes as $t\rightarrow 0,t<0$ , is the plane$z=0$ (this implies that the normal vector is discontinuous at $t=0$ and shows why we excluded points where $k=0$ ).
>
>d. Show that $\tau$ can be defined so that $\tau\equiv 0$ , even though $\alpha$ is not a plane curve.

**Proof:**
**a.** For $t>0$: $\alpha'(t)=(1,0,\frac{2}{t^3}e^{-1/t^2})$; for $t<0$: $\alpha'(t)=(1,\frac{2}{t^3}e^{-1/t^2},0)$; for $t=0$, since $\lim_{t \to 0} e^{-1/t^2}/|t|^n = 0$ for any $n$, we have
$$ \alpha'(0) = \lim_{t \to 0} \frac{\alpha(t)-\alpha(0)}{t} = (1,0,0). $$
Thus $\alpha$ is differentiable everywhere.

**b.** For $t>0$, $||\alpha'(t)||^2=1+4t^{-6}e^{-2/t^2}>0$; for $t<0$, $||\alpha'(t)||^2=1+4t^{-6}e^{-2/t^2}>0$; and $\alpha'(0)=(1,0,0)\neq 0$, so $\alpha$ is regular.

Now compute curvature $k(t)=\frac{||\alpha'(t)\times \alpha''(t)||}{||\alpha'(t)||^3}$. For $t>0$,
$$ \alpha'(t)=(1,0,2t^{-3}e^{-1/t^2}),\quad \alpha''(t)=(0,0,(-6t^{-4}+4t^{-6})e^{-1/t^2}), $$
$$ \alpha'(t)\times \alpha''(t)=(0, (6t^{-4}-4t^{-6})e^{-1/t^2}, 0), $$
so $||\alpha'(t)\times \alpha''(t)||=2t^{-6}|3t^2-2|e^{-1/t^2}$. Since $e^{-1/t^2}\neq0$, $k(t)=0$ iff $3t^2-2=0$, i.e., $t=\sqrt{2/3}$. For $t<0$, by symmetry, $k(t)=0$ iff $t=-\sqrt{2/3}$. Therefore, for $t\neq0,\pm\sqrt{2/3}$, $k(t)\neq0$. 

Moreover,
$$
\alpha'(0)=(1,0,0),\quad \alpha''(0)=\lim_{t \to 0} \frac{\alpha'(t)-\alpha'(0)}{t} = (0,0,0), 
$$
So $k(0)=0$.

**c.** The osculating plane at $t$ is spanned by $\alpha'(t)$ and $\alpha''(t)$ (or equivalently, by $T(t)$ and $N(t)$). For $t>0$,
$$ \alpha'(t)=(1,0,2t^{-3}e^{-1/t^2}),\quad \alpha''(t)=(0,0,(-6t^{-4}+4t^{-6})e^{-1/t^2}). $$
These vectors lie in the $xz$-plane ($y=0$). Their cross product is in the $y$-direction, so the normal vector to the osculating plane is parallel to $(0,1,0)$. As $t\to0^+$, $\alpha'(t)\to(1,0,0)$ and $\alpha''(t)\to(0,0,0)$, but the limiting plane is $y=0$.

For $t<0$,
$$ \alpha'(t)=(1,2t^{-3}e^{-1/t^2},0),\quad \alpha''(t)=(0,(-6t^{-4}+4t^{-6})e^{-1/t^2},0), $$
which lie in the $xy$-plane ($z=0$). Their cross product is in the $z$-direction, so the normal vector is parallel to $(0,0,1)$. As $t\to0^-$, the limiting osculating plane is $z=0$.

Thus the osculating plane (and hence the binormal or principal normal) is discontinuous at $t=0$.

**d.** Torsion $\tau$ is given by $\tau(t)=-\frac{(\alpha'\times\alpha'')\cdot\alpha'''}{||\alpha'\times\alpha''||^2}$ (by the next problem). For $t>0$, compute $\alpha'''(t)$:
$$ \alpha'''(t)=(0,0,\left[24t^{-5}-36t^{-7}+8t^{-9}\right]e^{-1/t^2}). $$
Then $(\alpha'\times\alpha'')\cdot\alpha'''=0$ because $\alpha'\times\alpha''$ is in the $y$-direction while $\alpha'''$ is in the $z$-direction. For $t<0$, similarly $(\alpha'\times\alpha'')\cdot\alpha'''=0$. For $t=0$, we have $\alpha''(0)=0$ thus $\tau(0)=0$.Thus $\tau(t)\equiv0$ for all $t$.

However, $\alpha$ is not a plane curve because: for $t>0$, the curve is in the $xz$-plane ($y=0$), and for $t<0$, it is in the $xy$-plane ($z=0$). But the curve is not contained in the intersection of these two planes.

>[!problem] Problem 8
>Let $\alpha: I\longrightarrow R^{3}$ be a regular parametrized curve (not necessarily by arc length) and let $\beta: J\longrightarrow R^{3}$ be a reparametrization of $\alpha(I)$ by the arc length $s=s(t)$, measured from $t_{0}\in I$ (see Remark 2). Let $t=t(s)$ be the inverse function of s and set $d\alpha/dt=\alpha^{\prime}$, $d^{2}\alpha/dt^{2}=\alpha^{\prime\prime}$, etc. Prove that
>
>a. $dt/ds=1/|\alpha^{\prime}|$, $d^{2}t/ds^{2}=-(\alpha^{\prime}\cdot\alpha^{\prime\prime}/|\alpha^{\prime}|^{4})$.
>
>b. The curvature of $\alpha$ at $t\in I$ is
>
>$$k(t)=\frac{\left|\alpha^{\prime}\times\alpha^{\prime\prime}\right|}{\left|\alpha^{\prime}\right|^{3}}.$$
>
>c. The torsion of $\alpha$ at $t\in I$ is
>
>$$\tau(t)=-\frac{\left(\alpha^{\prime}\times\alpha^{\prime\prime}\right)\cdot\alpha^{\prime\prime\prime}}{\left|\alpha^{\prime}\times\alpha^{\prime\prime}\right|^{2}}.$$
>
>d. If $\alpha: I\longrightarrow R^{2}$ is a plane curve $\alpha(t)=(x(t),y(t))$, the signed curvature (see Remark 1) of $\alpha$ at t is
>
>$$k(t)=\frac{x^{\prime} y^{\prime\prime}-x^{\prime\prime} y^{\prime}}{\left(\left(x^{\prime}\right)^{2}+\left(y^{\prime}\right)^{2}\right)^{3/2}}.$$

**Proof:**
**a.** Since $ds/dt = |\alpha'(t)|$, we have $dt/ds = 1/|\alpha'|$. Then
$$ \frac{d^2t}{ds^2} = \frac{d}{ds}\left(\frac{1}{|\alpha'|}\right) = \frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{1}{|\alpha'|}\right)\frac{dt}{ds} = -\frac{\alpha' \cdot \alpha''}{|\alpha'|^4}. $$

**b.** For $\beta(s)=\alpha(t(s))$, we have $\beta'(s)=\frac{\alpha'}{|\alpha'|}$. Then
$$ \beta''(s) = \frac{\alpha''}{|\alpha'|^2} - \frac{(\alpha' \cdot \alpha'')\alpha'}{|\alpha'|^4}. $$
Now, $k=|\beta''|$ and
$$ |\beta''|^2 = \frac{|\alpha''|^2}{|\alpha'|^4} - \frac{(\alpha' \cdot \alpha'')^2}{|\alpha'|^6} = \frac{|\alpha'|^2|\alpha''|^2 - (\alpha' \cdot \alpha'')^2}{|\alpha'|^6} = \frac{|\alpha' \times \alpha''|^2}{|\alpha'|^6}, $$
hence $k = \frac{|\alpha' \times \alpha''|}{|\alpha'|^3}$.

**c.** For arc-length $\beta$, torsion $\tau = -\frac{(\beta' \times \beta'') \cdot \beta'''}{|\beta' \times \beta''|^2}$. Compute
$$ \beta' \times \beta'' = \frac{\alpha' \times \alpha''}{|\alpha'|^3}. $$
Also, $(\beta' \times \beta'') \cdot \beta''' = \frac{(\alpha' \times \alpha'') \cdot \alpha'''}{|\alpha'|^6}$ (after simplification, noting that $(\alpha' \times \alpha'') \cdot \alpha'' = 0$). Therefore,
$$ \tau = -\frac{(\alpha' \times \alpha'') \cdot \alpha''' / |\alpha'|^6}{|\alpha' \times \alpha''|^2 / |\alpha'|^6} = -\frac{(\alpha' \times \alpha'') \cdot \alpha'''}{|\alpha' \times \alpha''|^2}. $$

**d.** For $\alpha(t)=(x(t),y(t))$, we have $\alpha'=(x',y')$, $\alpha''=(x'',y'')$, and $|\alpha'|=\sqrt{x'^2+y'^2}$. The cross product magnitude is $|\alpha' \times \alpha''| = |x'y'' - x''y'|$. Substituting into the formula from **b.** gives
$$ k(t) = \frac{|x'y'' - x''y'|}{(x'^2+y'^2)^{3/2}}. $$
The signed curvature includes the sign of the determinant, so
$$ k(t) = \frac{x'y'' - x''y'}{(x'^2+y'^2)^{3/2}}. $$

>[!problem] Problem 9
>Assume that $\tau(s)\neq 0$ and $k^{\prime}(s)\neq 0$ for all $s\in I$. Show that a necessary and sufficient condition for $\alpha(I)$ to lie on a sphere is that
>
>$$R^{2}+(R^{\prime})^{2}T^{2}=\text{const.}$$
>
>where $R=1/k$, $T=1/\tau$, and $R^{\prime}$ is the derivative of $R$ relative to $s$.

**Proof:**
($\Rightarrow$) Assume $\alpha(s)$ lies on a sphere with center $\mathbf{c}$ and radius $a$: $|\alpha-\mathbf{c}|^2 = a^2$.  
Differentiating and using Frenet equations gives:  
1. $(\alpha-\mathbf{c})\cdot T=0$,
2. $(\alpha-\mathbf{c})\cdot N=-R$,  
3. $(\alpha-\mathbf{c})\cdot B=-R'T$,  
where $R=1/k$, $T=1/\tau$. Thus $\alpha-\mathbf{c}=-RN-R'T\,B$ and  
$$|\alpha-\mathbf{c}|^2 = R^2 + (R')^2T^2 = a^2 = \text{const}.$$
($\Leftarrow$) Suppose $R^2+(R')^2T^2=C$ (constant). Define  
$$\mathbf{c} = \alpha + RN + R'T\,B.$$
Differentiating $\mathbf{c}$ and using $R=1/k$, $T=1/\tau$, together with the derivative of the given condition, yields $\mathbf{c}'=0$. Hence $\mathbf{c}$ is constant. Then  
$$|\alpha-\mathbf{c}|^2 = R^2+(R')^2T^2 = C,$$
so $\alpha$ lies on the sphere centered at $\mathbf{c}$ with radius $\sqrt{C}$.

>[!problem] Problem 10
>Let $\alpha:(a,b)\longrightarrow R^{2}$ be a regular parametrized plane curve. Assume that there exists $t_{0}$, $a<t_{0}<b$, such that the distance $|\alpha(t)|$ from the origin to the trace of $\alpha$ is maximal at $t=t_{0}$.
>
>Prove that the curvature $k$ of $\alpha$ at $t_{0}$ satisfies $|k(t_{0})|\geq1/|\alpha(t_{0})|$.

**Proof:**
Let $f(t) = |\alpha(t)|^2 = \alpha(t) \cdot \alpha(t)$. Since $|\alpha(t)|$ is maximal at $t_0$, we have $f'(t_0)=0$ and $f''(t_0) \le 0$.
First, $f'(t_0)=2\alpha(t_0)\cdot\alpha'(t_0)=0 \implies \alpha(t_0) \perp \alpha'(t_0)$.
Second, $f''(t_0)=2\big[|\alpha'(t_0)|^2 + \alpha(t_0)\cdot\alpha''(t_0)\big] \le 0$, so:
$$\alpha(t_0)\cdot\alpha''(t_0) \le -|\alpha'(t_0)|^2. \tag{1}$$
Let $\rho = |\alpha(t_0)| > 0$. Write $\alpha(t_0) = \rho N$, where $N$ is a unit vector orthogonal to the tangent direction (since $\alpha(t_0)$ is orthogonal to the tangent). Then (1) becomes:
$$\rho N \cdot \alpha''(t_0) \le -|\alpha'(t_0)|^2.$$
Hence, the component of $\alpha''(t_0)$ in the direction $N$ satisfies:
$$N \cdot \alpha''(t_0) \le -\frac{|\alpha'(t_0)|^2}{\rho}.$$
The signed curvature $k(t_0)$ is given by
$$k(t_0) = \frac{\alpha'(t_0) \times \alpha''(t_0)}{|\alpha'(t_0)|^3},$$
where the cross product in $\mathbb{R}^2$ yields a scalar. Since $\alpha'(t_0)$ is perpendicular to $N$, and $N \cdot \alpha''(t_0)$ is exactly the normal component of acceleration (up to sign), we have:
$$|k(t_0)| = \frac{|N \cdot \alpha''(t_0)|}{|\alpha'(t_0)|^2} \ge \frac{|\alpha'(t_0)|^2/\rho}{|\alpha'(t_0)|^2} = \frac{1}{\rho}.$$
Therefore, $|k(t_0)| \ge 1/|\alpha(t_0)|$.

>[!problem] Problem 11
>Show that the knowledge of the vector function $b=b(s)$ (binormal vector) of a curve $\alpha$, with nonzero torsion everywhere, determines the curvature $k(s)$ and the absolute value of the torsion $|\tau(s)|$ of $\alpha$.

**Proof:**
Since $b'(s) = \tau(s) n(s)$, we have $|\tau(s)| = |b'(s)|$, so $|\tau(s)|$ is determined.

Because $\tau(s) \neq 0$, we may write the principal normal as
   $$n(s) = \frac{b'(s)}{\tau(s)} = \frac{b'(s)}{|b'(s)|} \operatorname{sgn}(\tau(s)).$$
The tangent vector is $t(s) = n(s) \times b(s)$, so
$$t(s) = \frac{1}{|b'(s)|}\big(b'(s) \times b(s)\big) \operatorname{sgn}(\tau(s)).$$
Now, $k(s) = |t'(s)|$. After differentiation and simplification, one obtains
$$k(s) = \frac{|b'(s) \times b''(s)|}{|b'(s)|^3}.$$
Thus the function $b(s)$ alone determines both $k(s)$ and $|\tau(s)|$.

>[!problem] Problem 12
>**a.** Let $\alpha(s), s \in [0, l]$, be a plane simple closed curve. Assume that the curvature $k(s)$ satisfies $0 < k(s) \le c$, where $c$ is a constant (thus, $\alpha$ is less curved than a circle of radius $1/c$). Prove that
>
>$$ \text{length of } \alpha \ge \frac{2\pi}{c}. $$
>
>**b.** In part a, replace the assumption of being simple by $\alpha$ has rotation index $N$. Prove that
>
>$$ \text{length of } \alpha \ge \frac{2\pi N}{c}. $$

**Proof:**
**a.** For a simple closed plane curve $\alpha(s)$ of length $l$, parametrized by arc length $s\in[0,l]$, the total curvature is $2\pi$. That is,
$$\int_0^l k(s)\, ds = 2\pi.$$
Since $0 < k(s) \le c$ for all $s$, we have
$$2\pi = \int_0^l k(s)\, ds \le \int_0^l c\, ds = c \cdot l.$$
Thus $l \ge 2\pi / c$.

**b.** When the curve has rotation index $N$, the total curvature equals $2\pi N$:
$$\int_0^l k(s)\, ds = 2\pi N.$$
Again, using $k(s) \le c$,
$$2\pi N = \int_0^l k(s)\, ds \le c \cdot l,$$
so $l \ge 2\pi N / c$.
