>[!problem] Definitions
**$\sigma$-algebra**:
Let $\Omega$ be a non-empty set. A collection $\mathcal{F}$ of subsets of $\Omega$ is called a $\sigma$-algebra if it satisfies:
>1. $\Omega \in \mathcal{F}$
>2. If $A \in \mathcal{F}$, then $A^c \in \mathcal{F}$ (closed under complements)
>3. If $A_1, A_2, \dots \in \mathcal{F}$, then $\bigcup_{n=1}^\infty A_n \in \mathcal{F}$ (closed under countable unions)
>
>**Measurable Space**:
>A pair $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$ where $\Omega$ is a set and $\mathcal{F}$ is a $\sigma$-algebra on $\Omega$ is called a measurable space.
>
>**Probability Measure**:
>Let $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$ be a measurable space. A function $P: \mathcal{F} \to [0,1]$ is called a probability measure if:
>1. $P(\Omega) = 1$
>2. For any countable collection of pairwise disjoint sets $\{A_n\}_{n=1}^\infty \subseteq \mathcal{F}$, $P\left(\bigcup_{n=1}^\infty A_n\right) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty P(A_n)$ (countable additivity)
>
>**Probability Space**:
>A triple $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ where $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$ is a measurable space and $P$ is a probability measure is called a probability space.
>
>**Borel $\sigma$-algebra**:
>The Borel $\sigma$-algebra on $\mathbb{R}$, denoted $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$, is the $\sigma$-algebra generated by all open intervals in $\mathbb{R}$.
>
**Random Variable**:
>Let $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ be a probability space. A function $X: \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$ is called a random variable if for every Borel set $B \in \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$, we have $X^{-1}(B) \in \mathcal{F}$. That is, $X$ is $(\mathcal{F}, \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}))$-measurable.

>[!problem] Theorem
>**$X^{-1}(\mathcal{F})$ is a $\sigma$-algebra**
>
>Let $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ be a probability space and $X: \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$ be a random variable. Then $X^{-1}(\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}))$ is a $\sigma$-algebra on $\Omega$.

**Proof:**
1. **$\Omega \in\mathcal{F}$**: Since $\mathbb{R} \in \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$, we have $X^{-1}(\mathbb{R}) = \Omega \in X^{-1}(\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}))$.

2. **Closed under complements**: Let $A \in X^{-1}(\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}))$. Then there exists $B \in \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$ such that $A = X^{-1}(B)$. Since $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$ is a $\sigma$-algebra, $B^c \in \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$. Then $X^{-1}(B^c) = (X^{-1}(B))^c = A^c \in X^{-1}(\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}))$.

3. **Closed under countable unions**: Let $\{A_n\}_{n=1}^\infty \subseteq X^{-1}(\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}))$. Then for each $n$, there exists $B_n \in \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$ such that $A_n = X^{-1}(B_n)$. Since $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$ is a $\sigma$-algebra, $\bigcup_{n=1}^\infty B_n \in \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$. Then $X^{-1}\left(\bigcup_{n=1}^\infty B_n\right) = \bigcup_{n=1}^\infty X^{-1}(B_n) = \bigcup_{n=1}^\infty A_n \in X^{-1}(\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}))$.

Therefore, $X^{-1}(\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}))$ is a $\sigma$-algebra on $\Omega$.

>[!problem] Theorem
>Let $X \sim N(\mu_X, \sigma_X^2)$ and $Y \sim N(\mu_Y, \sigma_Y^2)$ be jointly normal random variables. Then $X$ and $Y$ are uncorrelated (i.e., $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=\operatorname{Corr}(X,Y)=0$ or $E[XY]=E[X]E[Y]$) if and only if they are independent.

**Proof**:
Independent $\Rightarrow$ Uncorrelated holds for any distributions. We only have to show Uncorrelated $\Rightarrow$ Independent for normal distributoins.

The joint MGF of $(X,Y)$ is $M_{X,Y}(t_1,t_2) = E[e^{t_1X + t_2Y}]$, substituting and simplifying gives:
$$M_{X,Y}(t_1,t_2) = \exp\left(\mu_X t_1 + \mu_Y t_2 + \frac{1}{2}(\sigma_X^2 t_1^2 + 2\rho\sigma_X\sigma_Y t_1t_2 + \sigma_Y^2 t_2^2)\right)$$
where $\rho = \operatorname{Corr}(X,Y)$.

Since $X$ and $Y$ are uncorrelated, $\rho = 0$. The joint MGF becomes:
$$M_{X,Y}(t_1,t_2) = \exp\left(\mu_X t_1 + \mu_Y t_2 + \frac{1}{2}(\sigma_X^2 t_1^2 + \sigma_Y^2 t_2^2)\right)$$
The MGFs of $X$ and $Y$ are:
$$M_X(t_1) = E[e^{t_1X}] = \exp\left(\mu_X t_1 + \frac{1}{2}\sigma_X^2 t_1^2\right)$$
$$M_Y(t_2) = E[e^{t_2Y}] = \exp\left(\mu_Y t_2 + \frac{1}{2}\sigma_Y^2 t_2^2\right)$$
We observe that:
$$M_{X,Y}(t_1,t_2) = M_X(t_1) \cdot M_Y(t_2)$$
According to the uniqueness theorem of MGFs, $X$ and $Y$ are independent.
