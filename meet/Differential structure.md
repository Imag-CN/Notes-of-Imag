> [!problem]
> Let $M$ be a topological manifold. Given an $C^r$-atlas $\mathcal{A}$ on $M$, prove that there exists a unique $C^r$-differentiable structure (i.e. a maximal atlas) $\mathcal{D}$ on $M$ that contains $\mathcal{A}$.

**Proof:**
Let $\mathcal{A} = \{(U_\alpha, \phi_\alpha)\}_{\alpha \in I}$ be a $C^r$-atlas on $M$. Define $\mathcal{D}$ to be the set of all charts $(V, \psi)$ on $M$ that are $C^r$-compatible with every chart in $\mathcal{A}$, i.e., for all $\alpha \in I$, the transition maps  
$$
\phi_\alpha \circ \psi^{-1} : \psi(U_\alpha \cap V) \to \phi_\alpha(U_\alpha \cap V)
$$  
and  
$$
\psi \circ \phi_\alpha^{-1} : \phi_\alpha(U_\alpha \cap V) \to \psi(U_\alpha \cap V)
$$  
are $C^r$-diffeomorphisms.

Since all charts in $\mathcal{A}$ are mutually $C^r$-compatible, $\mathcal{A} \subseteq \mathcal{D}$. $\mathcal{D}$ cover $M$ because $\mathcal{A}$ does.

All charts in $\mathcal{D}$ are $C^r$-compatible: if $(V, \psi), (W, \xi) \in \mathcal{D}$, then for any $a\in V \cap W$, 
let $(U,\phi)\in\mathcal{A}$ such that $a\in U$. Then
$$
\xi \circ\psi ^{-1}=\xi \circ(\phi^{-1}\circ\phi)\circ\psi ^{-1}=(\xi \circ\phi^{-1})\circ(\phi\circ\psi ^{-1}):\psi(U\cap V\cap W)\to\xi(U\cap V\cap W)
$$
and
$$
\psi \circ \xi^{-1}= \psi\circ(\phi^{-1}\circ\phi)\circ\xi ^{-1}=(\xi \circ\phi^{-1})\circ(\phi\circ\psi ^{-1}):\psi(U\cap V\cap W)\to\xi(U\cap V\cap W)
$$
are $C^r$-smooth at $a$.
By the arbitrariness of $a$,
$$
\xi \circ\psi ^{-1}:\psi(V\cap W)\to\xi( V\cap W)
$$
and
$$
\psi \circ \xi^{-1}:\psi( V\cap W)\to\xi( V\cap W)
$$
are $C^r$-diffeomorphisms.

Maximality and uniqueness are derived by definition.

>[!tip]
>$\mathcal{D}$ is called the differential structure generated by $\mathcal{A}$, denoted as $(\mathbb{R},\mathcal{A})$.

>[!example]
>
> Give two examples of distinct $C^1$-differentiable structures on $\mathbb{R}$.

Let $\mathcal{A}_1 = \{(\mathbb{R}, \mathrm{id})\}$ be the atlas with the identity chart, $\mathcal{D}_1$ be the differentiable structure generated by $\mathcal{A}_1$.

Let $\phi(x) = x^3$ and $\mathcal{A}_2 = \{(\mathbb{R}, \phi)\}$. Let $\mathcal{D}_2$ be the differentiable structure generated by $\mathcal{A}_2$.

The transition map between these structures is $\mathrm{id} \circ \phi^{-1}(x) = x^{1/3}$. This map is not $C^1$ at $x=0$, so $\mathcal{D}_1 \neq \mathcal{D}_2$.

> [!problem]
> Prove that all $C^1$ differentiable structures on $\mathbb{R}$ are diffeomorphic.

**Proof:**
It is sufficient to show that any $C^1$ structure $(\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{A})$ is $C^1$-diffeomorphic to the standard structure $(\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{A}_{\text{std}})$ where $\mathcal{A}_{\text{std}} = \{(\mathbb{R}, \operatorname{id})\}$.

Take a chart $(U, \phi)$ in $\mathcal{A}$ containing $0$ with $\phi: U \to \phi(U) \subset \mathbb{R}$ a $C^1$-diffeomorphism. Define $F: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ by:

$$
F(x) = \int_0^x \frac{1}{\phi'(\phi^{-1}(t))}  dt
$$
Since $\phi$ is a $C^1$-diffeomorphism, $\phi'$ is continuous and nowhere zero, so the integrand is continuous and $F$ is $C^1$.

We have
$$
F'(x) = \frac{1}{\phi'(\phi^{-1}(x))} \neq 0 \quad \text{for all } x \in \mathbb{R}
$$
and $F'(x)$ is continuous, so we may assume $F'(x)>0$.
Thus $F$ is strictly monotonic and injective.

As $x \to \pm\infty$, $F(x) \to \pm\infty$, so $F$ is surjective.

Thus $F: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ is a $C^1$-diffeomorphism.

Consider $F: (\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{A}) \to (\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{A}_{\text{std}})$. For any $p \in \mathbb{R}$ and chart $(V, \psi)$ in $\mathcal{A}$ containing $p$, the local representation:
$$
F \circ \psi^{-1}: \psi(V) \to \mathbb{R}
$$
is $C^1$ by construction. Similarly, $F^{-1}$ is $C^1$ in local coordinates.

Hence, all $C^1$ structures on $\mathbb{R}$ are $C^1$-diffeomorphic.
